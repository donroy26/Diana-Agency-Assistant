# Property Research Rules

## Before Anything Else

Read Deal Type from deal-file.md. Buyer mode and seller mode produce completely different outputs. Do not start writing until you know which one applies.

Also read the full Lead Card. The research must be calibrated to this client's specific preferences, budget, and timeline, not generic Austin market commentary.

If research-brief.md already exists in the deal folder: do not overwrite silently. Ask the agent what changed (new area, adjusted budget, expanded search?) before rewriting. When you do rewrite, add a "Refreshed:" date line at the top of the file below the original date, and update the summary in deal-file.md.

---

## Buyer Mode

When Deal Type is "buyer," produce research-brief.md with:

1. **Neighborhood analysis** — evaluate the target areas from the Lead Card against the client's stated priorities (school districts, walkability, commute, neighborhood vibe). Be specific to Austin: cite actual neighborhoods by name, note what's different between them.

2. **Comparable sales** — recent sales (last 90 days preferred) in the target area, in the client's price range. List price, sold price, days on market, and key features. At minimum 3 comps.

3. **School ratings** — only include this section if school district was mentioned in the Lead Card. If it was not mentioned, omit the section entirely. Do not include generic ratings or infer a preference.

4. **Market conditions** — for the specific price range in the target area: Is it a buyer's or seller's market right now? Average days on market? Are homes selling above or below list? How competitive is this segment?

5. **Budget reality check** — does the budget get what the client wants in their target area? If not, say so clearly and describe the tradeoffs: budget up, area out, or expectations adjusted.

6. **Timeline check** — only include this section if a hard deadline is present in the Lead Card. State whether the deadline is achievable given average days on market in the target area plus a standard offer-to-close timeline (typically 30–45 days in Austin). If the timeline is tight or at risk, flag it explicitly and state what it means: the client needs to be ready to move fast, or the timeline may not be achievable in the target area.

## Seller Mode

When Deal Type is "seller," produce research-brief.md with:

1. **Active comparable listings** — what else is on the market right now in the same area and price range. List price, days on market, key features. This is the competition.

2. **Recently sold comparables** — last 90 days. Sold price, original list price, days on market. Minimum 3 comps.

3. **List price range recommendation** — based on comps, state a supported range. Do not give a single number without rationale.

4. **Seller expectation check** — explicitly state whether the seller's price expectation (from Lead Card) is above, within, or below the supported comp range. If it is above: say so directly, state the gap, and describe the risk (days on market, likely price reductions). Do not bury this finding inside the price range section.

5. **MLS positioning paragraph** — one short paragraph (3–5 sentences) describing what makes this property competitive. Written for the MLS description, not for the client. Factual, specific to the property's actual features.

6. **Market conditions** — same as buyer mode but from the seller's perspective: How fast are similar homes selling? What concessions are sellers making? Is this a good time to list in this segment?

---

## Rules for Both Modes

- Always write full output to research-brief.md in the deal folder. Do not put research content inline in deal-file.md.
- Always append a one-paragraph summary to deal-file.md referencing research-brief.md: what you found, what it means for this client, and what the recommended next step is.
- Always set Stage to "researched" after writing.
- Never recommend a specific offer price — that is 05_offer_navigator's job once the client is ready to move.
- Never write anything client-facing.
- If the agent gives you incomplete information, ask for what you need before writing. Do not produce a generic brief. Examples of blockers:
  - Buyer: location preferences flagged "unknown," budget flagged "unknown," or deal type missing
  - Seller: no property address, no property description, no price expectation (even a vague one)
